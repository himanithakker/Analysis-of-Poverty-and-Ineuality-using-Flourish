Poverty & Inequality in the U.S.

The website looks into the intricacies of demographic and residential characteristics that shaped up trends in poverty distribution and income inequality within the U.S. over the years 2010-2020. Insights are gained from visualizations that bring into limelight dominant factors that plague financial equality of individuals, household types and families residing within the boundaries of the U.S.

Executive summary:

Analysis:

The data for analysis has been derived from United States Census Bureau library publications (https://www.census.gov/topics/income-poverty/poverty.html) focused on “Income and Poverty in the United States” from years 2010-2020.


Conclusions:

  • The Southern region/ states within the U.S. have the highest prevalence of poor living below the poverty level


  • Race does play a significant role in determining the overall percentage of poor living below poverty. Black people are the most downtrodden


  • Education, nativity, residence and age also play important roles in determining poverty trends. Least educated, people living inside principal cities and those with disabilities are the most vulnerable


  • A higher percentage of females live below poverty level compared to males. This highlights disparity in income and possible workplace bias


  • Covid-19 played a significant role in shaping up the poverty trends across the U.S. Although, there had been a continuous downward trend in the same from 2010 onwards, a recent spike has been noticed due to job losses and access to limited job opportunities (a trend that has been fueled by the pandemic)



Understanding Poverty In US

The United States has undoubtedly had an impressive economic growth over the last century. Once established as the land of the free, the nation has provided the economic stage for countless individuals to craft out their slice of the American dream. More questionable is the fact that about 11% of Americans live in poverty, despite the national prosperity. Also, the acceptance that poverty for some is an unfortunate consequence of free markets is present among a strong majority of population.

The prevalence of such a system means that inequality is higher in the United States than in many other developed countries around the world. There is a direct link between one’s personal income and poverty: a person who earns a small amount is more likely to live in poverty. However, others’ incomes also play a role in the likelihood of someone being in poverty. In 2020, 9.4% of American households had an annual income equal to or less than US$ 15,000 per year. For such households, general inflation is a major concern.

It is worth noting here that the application of many otherwise universal definitions of poverty are not aptly applicable for high income, and therefore high price, economies like the United States. In the U.S., the national poverty is calculated by tripling the inflation-adjusted cost of a minimum food diet in 1963, and adjusting for family size, composition, and the age of the householder. In relation to extreme poverty, those with a level of income less than 50% of the poverty level are considered to be in deep poverty.

It is of a relatively high concern for policy makers and concerned observers that there is a sizeable difference in poverty rates when analyzed in terms of ethnic communities. 19.5% of the Black people and 17% of Hispanic people in the U.S. lived below poverty line in 2020; corresponding figures for all races cumulatively being 11.4%. These statistics are indeed concerning as they suggest that institutional and social problems beyond economic factors face certain groups in the U.S. Even though social security is race neutral (at least at the bureaucratic level), there is a notable racial imbalance across the overall scenario.

Demographic characteristics shaping up poverty distribution


The above visual aptly showcases that poverty is more pronounced among the black, and they make up the majority (19.5%) of people living "below poverty" when race is taken into consideration. It hardly comes as a surprise that people with no high school diploma comprise a major chunk of the below poverty level section (24.7%) when educational attainment is considered. A similar trend can be noted for the section without "significant work experience" when work experience is the focus characteristic. Non-citizens take up the top spot when nativity is considered, something that can be attributed to the lack of access to social security benefits. Poverty is more prevalent among people living inside principal cities, probably due to the very high living expenses that need to be incurred therein. People with a disability also seem to suffer more, due to the restrictions imposed on their working abilities. It is also seen that the percentage of females living below poverty exceeds the same for males (12.6% and 10.2% respectively), indicating inequality in income levels.

Comparison of demographic and residential characteristic parameters (% change) from 2015 to 2020


The visual presented above highlights a few notable quantifiable changes seen across demographic and residential demographic characteristics from 2015 to 2020. The overall percentage of people living below poverty has reduced during the last half decade from 13.5% to 11.4%. Significant change can also be seen in the percentage of black people who were living below poverty in 2015 compared to the 2020 figures (reduction from 24.1% to 19.5%). An overall downward trend can be noticed for females, though it is still significantly higher compared to males, suggesting disparity in income levels. The western region of U.S. has seen the most progress in reducing the percentage of people living below poverty, when compared to the other regions.

While the percentage of poor living below poverty level has seen a downward trend overall, an anomaly can be noted among people who have been to high school, but have not been to college. In the current scenario, the percentage of people living below poverty has gone up from 12.9% to 13.2%. This indicates a trend that certain job categories are getting more and more specialized and hence are requiring college attendance as a pre-requisite.

Effect of family size on poverty thresholds


Family size does impact poverty threshold, as portrayed in the above visual. Contrary to normal understanding, a marked difference in the weighted average thresholds for "two people householder under age 65" and "two people householder aged 65 and older" can be noted. The same can be attributed to "medicare" and other "senior citizen benefits" that can be availed only after 65.

Overall, the weighted average thresholds show a positive trend when plotted against the number of members in the family i.e., they increase with an increase in the number of family members.

Inequality Indicator - The Gini Index

The Gini Index is also known as the Gini Coefficient which is used to measure the income distribution across a population. The coefficient value ranges from 0 (0%) to 1(100%), where 0 represents perfect equality and 1 represents inequality. A country where each resident has the same remuneration would have a Gini coefficient of 0, similarly, in a country where one resident earned all the remunerations would have a Gini Coefficient of 1.

U.S Gini Index for households by Race and Hispanic origin of householder (2010- 2020)

The above-visualized graph shows us how Gini Index for households varies by various races. The categories are White Alone, White Alone Not Hispanic, Black A.O.I.C, Black Alone, Asian A.O.I.C, Asian alone, and Hispanic(any race). White alone, Black alone, and Asian alone refers to people who reported White, Black, or Asian and did not report any other race category. Hispanic (any race) refers to people asking a question if the person is Spanish, Hispanic, or Latino. Black AOIC refers to people who reported their race as Black and with a combination of other race groups, similarly Asian AOIC refers to people who reported their race as Asian with a combination of other race groups.

From the above graph, we can conclude that Gini Index for the "Black Alone" race is Highest (0.51) when compared to "All races" (0.49) in the United States.
